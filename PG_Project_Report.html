01 — Executive Summary

What This Model Says About P&G

This model values Procter & Gamble (NYSE: PG) using a discounted cash flow methodology based on 6 years of historical financials and 5 years of projected free cash flows. The analysis suggests P&G is moderately overvalued at current market prices.

Key Finding

The Gordon Growth Model implies a share price of $126.15 and the Exit Multiple method implies $136.15 — both significantly below the current market price of $165.00. This suggests P&G may be overvalued by approximately 17–24%.

Critical Caveat

A model is a framework, not a trading signal. Nearly 80% of the enterprise value comes from the terminal value assumption. Changing the terminal growth rate by just 50 basis points swings the implied share price by $15–20. The value of this model is not the output — it's the discipline of building it.

02 — Company Overview

Why Procter & Gamble?

P&G is the world's largest consumer goods company by market capitalisation, with a portfolio of brands including Tide, Pampers, Gillette, and Oral-B. It generates over $84 billion in annual revenue across 180+ countries.

I chose P&G for three reasons: its financial statements are complex enough to test a genuine three-statement model (multiple segments, significant intangibles, currency translation effects), its 10-K filings are publicly available with detailed disclosure, and its stable business model makes revenue forecasting feasible without speculative assumptions.

03 — Model Architecture

How the Statements Connect

The model consists of four worksheets — Assumptions, Statements (IS + BS + CFS), Statement of Equity, and DCF — all cross-linked so that changing any single assumption cascades through every output.

The Reconciliation Challenge

The hardest part of this project wasn't calculating WACC or terminal value. It was making the Balance Sheet balance. Every new line item I added would break something downstream. A working capital change wouldn't flow to cash flow. A depreciation figure wouldn't reconcile to the asset schedule. I had to trace every formula, cell by cell, until the Balance Check row returned zero across all five forecast years.

08 — Sensitivity Analysis

How Assumptions Drive the Output

The sensitivity table tests implied share price across 35 combinations of WACC (5.5%–8.5%) and terminal growth rate (1.5%–3.5%). Green cells indicate scenarios where P&G appears undervalued relative to the $165 market price; red cells indicate overvaluation.

7×5 matrix: WACC (5.5%–8.5%) vs Terminal Growth Rate (1.5%–3.5%). At the base case WACC of 7.3% and terminal growth of 2.5%, the model implies ~$126 per share.

The Takeaway

Only at very low WACC assumptions (below 6%) combined with high terminal growth rates (above 3%) does the model support the current market price. Under most reasonable assumption combinations, P&G appears overvalued — suggesting the market may be pricing in either lower risk or higher growth than our model assumes.

09 — Limitations & Honest Assessment

What This Model Doesn't Capture

Every financial model is a simplification of reality. Here's what this one gets wrong, and why it matters:

Segment-Level Analysis

P&G operates across 5 segments (Beauty, Grooming, Health Care, Fabric & Home Care, Baby/Feminine/Family Care) with different growth profiles and margins. This model uses consolidated figures, which may mask segment-level dynamics.

Currency Effects

With over 50% of revenue from international markets, P&G's reported earnings are significantly affected by FX translation. The model forecasts in USD and doesn't account for currency movements.

Static Capital Structure

WACC is calculated based on the current capital structure and assumed constant. In reality, P&G actively manages its debt profile and share buybacks alter the equity base over time.

Terminal Value Dominance

79% of enterprise value comes from the terminal value — a perpetuity assumption that is inherently uncertain. This is a known limitation of all DCF models for mature, stable businesses.

10 — What I Learned

Beyond the Numbers

The technical skills this project developed — WACC calculation, terminal value estimation, sensitivity analysis — are important but not the most valuable outcome.

The real learning was in reconciliation discipline. Every time I added a new assumption or changed a formula, something downstream would break. I developed a systematic approach: isolate the section, trace every cross-reference, test each formula individually, and only move forward when the output is clean. That process is identical to what happens in daily NAV calculation in fund accounting — where every number must reconcile before it goes to the client.

This project also taught me the difference between precision and accuracy. A model with 607 formulas and zero errors is precise. Whether its output reflects reality depends entirely on the quality of the assumptions. That humility — knowing that your model is only as good as your inputs — is something I'll carry into any analytical role.
